# scTMEatlas

## Stress hormone-responsive neutrophils promote tumor microenvironment immunosuppression
Psychological and physical stress is common among cancer patients and is associated with poor survival. Stress elevates glucocorticoids (GCs), but the global effect of GCs on the tumor microenvironment (TME) remains largely unknown. We used a neutrophil-preserving single-cell RNA-seq workflow to analyze 377 new clinical and 242 in-house samples and integrated them with 480 public samples, generating a pan-cancer atlas of 1,099 samples across 26 cancer types. From over 10 million cells, we identified 119 cell subpopulations, including 19 neutrophil subtypes. The atlas enables patient stratification into six TME subtypes, including a previously unrecognized GC-responsive “Steroid” subtype with poor prognosis and limited immunotherapy response. TME-Steroid is featured with a newly identified GC-responsive neutrophil lineage that includes tumor-associated FKBP5⁺, along with circulating IL1R2⁺ neutrophils that serve as a biomarker. Mechanistically, GCs enhanced neutrophil chemotaxis and degranulation while suppressing inflammatory chemokine production, impairing T cell infiltration and cytotoxicity. Our study links stress hormone signaling to tumor immunosuppression through a neutrophil-mediated mechanism.
